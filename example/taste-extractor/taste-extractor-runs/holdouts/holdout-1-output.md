# Taste profile — seven-minute contemporary dance duet

## Job record

- Source artifact: the complete timestamped evidence packet in the holdout prompt, including one rehearsal note.
- Source medium: contemporary dance duet with sound, light, spatial composition, and a seven-minute temporal arc.
- Downstream target: a repeatable 45-minute architecture-decision meeting for 5–7 people, plus an implementation-ready facilitator handoff.
- Inspected: every timestamped observation and the rehearsal note supplied in the packet.
- Not inspected: the dance itself, video, still frames, audio, choreographic score, performer or choreographer interviews beyond the supplied note, audience response, or any external account of the work.
- Evidence boundary: source-specific observations below are limited to packet-stated facts. Claims about what those facts do are labeled as inferences.
- User constraints: do not search beyond the packet; distinguish observation, inference, invariant, and adaptable choice; preserve portable taste without dance, theatre, or themed imitation; include concrete yes/no implementation checks.
- Desired fidelity: high fidelity to relational structure and temporal logic; zero fidelity required for literal movement, staging, sound, or dance terminology.

## Taste thesis

Make consequential agreement legible by moving from spare, reciprocal transformation through deliberately withheld closure and visible recovery, while holding complex individual analysis to one shared path and ending with an explicit decision but no performance of unanimity.

## Transfer layers

### Source content — do not transfer

- The two performers, their identities, or any implied narrative.
- Exact steps, weight shifts, phrases, catches, walking, contact, exits, poses, or spatial diagonals.
- The exact seven-minute chronology, the eighteen/eight/four/six-second values, or a mechanically scaled timing ratio.
- Cool stage light, a bare stage as a visual set, dry clicks, a sustained tone, sound cuts, or audible exhalation.
- “Duet,” “unison,” “catch,” “choreography,” “phrase,” or similar dance language as names for meeting activities.

### Surface manifestations — adapt deliberately

- Visual sparseness may become one visible decision canvas and no presentation deck, because that reduces competing signals; it must not become lack of preparation or evidence.
- Stillness and sonic gaps may become silent read/write periods with explicit tasks; silence is not copied for mood.
- Passing movement may become a structured response relay; participants do not imitate gestures or physically pass an object.
- Near-contact may become explicit conditional support before commitment; it must not become artificial indecision.
- Recovery from the late catch may become a stress test or response to genuine late information; no fake accident is staged.
- The shared walking path may become a fixed architecture-decision record; nobody needs to walk or change seats.
- Role reversal may become steelmanning and ownership exchange; nobody performs another participant as a character.
- Separate exits may become differentiated follow-through commitments after a common close; physical exit routes are irrelevant.

### Portable principles — preserve

1. Low-stimulus entry with an active, transformed response.
2. Reciprocal variation with bounded dominance.
3. Scarce, exact convergence followed by protected reflection.
4. Deliberately delayed closure that exposes decision boundaries.
5. Visible disruption and in-sequence recovery.
6. Dense parallel work attached to one legible common spine.
7. Compressed role reversal before a quiet, differentiated close.
8. Functional restraint: every ritual must change timing, relation, balance, evidence, or the decision record.

## Signature principles

### 1. Respond by transforming

Every substantive response must preserve one recognizable part of the preceding proposition while changing a relevant assumption, scale, direction, criterion, or consequence. Paraphrase-only agreement is not a contribution. This is supported by T1 and T2.

### 2. Make convergence scarce and exact

Alignment is not “same preferred option.” It requires the same option, materially equivalent conditions, and the same acknowledged principal tradeoff. It is checked once provisionally and once at decision close at most; when found, it is marked rather than celebrated. This is supported by T3 and T10.

### 3. Approach the choice before taking it

Participants state the conditions under which they would support and reject each viable option before the decision owner commits. The point is to reveal the last meaningful gaps, not to prolong debate. This is supported by T4.

### 4. Let the break alter the record

The leading option must meet a credible failure scenario or genuine late constraint. If something breaks, the old state remains visible, the changed assumption is named, and the recovery becomes part of the rationale. This is supported by T6 and T10.

### 5. Keep one slow spine under dense analysis

Participants may analyze different architecture qualities concurrently, but every finding must land in a shared sequence: constraint, evidence or assumption, tradeoff, failure mode, recovery or reversibility, owner. This is supported by T7.

### 6. Reverse epistemic roles, then compress

The initial critic must state the strongest current case for the leading option; the initial proposer must state the strongest remaining reason to reject or condition it. The replay is shorter than the opening exchange and introduces no theatrical impersonation. This is supported by T8.

### 7. Close together without manufacturing unanimity

The meeting ends at one explicit process boundary, but participants may retain different views and leave with different responsibilities. The record, not a consensus performance, establishes closure. This is supported by T9 and T10.

## Productive tensions

- Sparse, not underprepared.
- Reciprocal, not mechanically equal-turn.
- Recognizable, not repetitive.
- Exact in agreement, not rigid in wording.
- Tense enough to expose boundaries, not adversarial.
- Decisive, not prematurely convergent.
- Visibly recoverable, not artificially chaotic.
- Dense, not scattered.
- Quiet, not ambiguous.
- Role-reversed, not role-played.
- Restrained, not affectless.
- Explicitly owned, not consensus-dependent.

## Invariants

These survive every implementation:

- The meeting uses one shared decision question and one shared decision record as its common spine.
- The first discussion phase requires transformed responses: retain, change, because, consequence.
- The facilitator prevents one person or one side from taking a fourth uninterrupted exchange in the same thread.
- Exact convergence is treated as exceptional and is defined by option plus conditions plus accepted tradeoff, not by a show of hands.
- Any exact convergence checkpoint is followed by assigned silent inspection before discussion resumes.
- Conditions-to-support, conditions-to-reject, and flip evidence are recorded before the final call.
- A credible disruption or failure scenario is processed visibly; the group does not erase the prior state or restart the meeting.
- The densest analysis stays attached to one fixed decision-record sequence.
- A shorter, role-reversed replay occurs before the owner decides.
- The meeting ends with a recorded `choose`, `defer`, or `escalate` outcome and differentiated ownership, without forced unanimity or a ceremonial closing round.
- Every protocol element must change evidence, timing, relation, decision balance, or the decision record. Decorative activity is removed.
- No literal dance, theatrical, musical, spatial, or costume element is introduced.

## Degrees of freedom

These may adapt without losing the taste:

- The collaboration tool, room, remote/hybrid format, and visual layout of the decision record.
- Exact pause length within the stated agenda bands, provided silence retains an assigned job and the meeting remains 45 minutes.
- Participant speaking order; the facilitator may route by affected expertise rather than seating order.
- Role combinations: with five people, facilitator and recorder may be combined; with six or seven, they should usually be separate. The decision owner remains distinct from the facilitator.
- Architecture-analysis lanes. Use only dimensions relevant to the decision, such as reliability, security, data, integration, performance, cost, migration, operability, or delivery.
- The organization’s legitimate decision-authority model. The protocol can support a single accountable owner, advice process, or documented escalation, but authority must be explicit before the meeting.
- The number of viable options, from two to four, including status quo when it is genuinely viable.
- Exact vocabulary for the response syntax, provided it still separates retained premise, change, reason, and consequence.
- Accessibility accommodations, including written participation, screen-reader-compatible records, extra read time, or an assistant recorder. Shrink readouts before removing the recovery or decision phases.
- The failure scenario. Use genuine late evidence if it arrives; otherwise use the strongest plausible architecture failure supported by available facts. Do not manufacture drama.
- The final status. A correct defer or escalation with owner and deadline is preferable to a fake choice unsupported by evidence.

## Anti-patterns and originality guardrails

Reject the implementation if any of the following is true:

- It uses movement, poses, music, clicks, lighting, stage directions, timed breathing, physical distance, “unison,” “catch,” or other dance-themed devices.
- It mechanically scales seven source minutes into forty-five target minutes.
- It calls ordinary round-robin discussion “reciprocity” while allowing paraphrase-only responses.
- It makes every person speak equally regardless of relevant expertise or decision authority. Bounded dominance is not expertise denial.
- It treats silence as atmosphere or awkward waiting rather than assigning reading, writing, inspection, or correction.
- It runs repeated votes or emoji polls and calls their frequency “alignment.”
- It merges close but materially different support conditions to create a false consensus.
- It uses delayed closure as an excuse not to decide by the defined deadline.
- It invents a fake mishap, ambushes an option owner, or publicly dramatizes a participant’s error to simulate visible recovery.
- It deletes or hides the destabilized version instead of preserving what changed and why.
- It creates many independent analysis documents without one stable synthesis spine.
- It turns role reversal into impersonation, forced advocacy, or a winner/loser debate.
- It equates a quiet ending with an incomplete record, vague next steps, or no accountable owner.
- It adds a flourish that does not change evidence, timing, relationship, decision balance, or the record.
- It copies source-specific language or source sequence labels into participant-facing materials.

# Production handoff — 45-minute architecture-decision protocol

## Outcome contract

At minute 43 the decision owner must record exactly one status:

1. `CHOOSE` — a selected option with conditions, accepted tradeoffs, accountable implementation owner, first action, and review trigger;
2. `DEFER` — a specifically named evidence gap, evidence owner, due date, and default action if the deadline is missed; or
3. `ESCALATE` — the authority or cross-boundary issue requiring escalation, the options and disagreement to carry forward, escalation owner, and escalation date.

`DEFER` and `ESCALATE` are explicit decisions. “Continue discussion,” “needs more thought,” and “circle back” are invalid outcomes.

## Pre-meeting gate

The facilitator checks these inputs at least one working day before the meeting:

- One answerable architecture decision question.
- One named decision owner with authority to choose, defer, or escalate.
- Why the decision is needed now and what happens if it is not made.
- Two to four viable options, including status quo if applicable.
- Non-negotiable system and organizational constraints.
- Links to available evidence; assumptions and unknowns visibly labeled.
- The decision’s reversibility, blast radius, and latest responsible decision date.
- Relevant architecture qualities and stakeholders represented among 5–7 attendees.

If the question, owner, authority, options, or minimum evidence is missing, do not use the 45 minutes for an open-ended presentation. Convert the event into a preparation session with a named owner and deadline, or reschedule.

## Roles

- **Decision owner:** makes and owns the final status. Must not facilitate.
- **Facilitator:** enforces the sequence, response syntax, silence, and time boundaries. Does not advocate an option while facilitating.
- **Recorder:** maintains the single shared record and preserves before/after revisions. May be combined with facilitator for a five-person meeting.
- **Context proposer:** states the initial proposition and evidence. May also be the decision owner if authority genuinely overlaps, but receives no extra discussion privileges.
- **Architecture participants:** supply relevant evidence and analysis across the applicable quality dimensions.
- **Challenge lead:** introduces the strongest evidence-grounded failure scenario in the recovery phase. This role can be held by any architecture participant and does not mean permanent opposition.

Use one accessible document visible to all participants. Do not use a presentation deck during the meeting. Prepare these fields:

## Facilitator runbook: exactly 45 minutes

### 00:00–03:00 — Frame and private orientation

**Facilitator action**

1. Show only the shared decision record.
2. Say: “We are deciding: `[decision question]`. `[decision owner]` owns the call. By minute 43 the record will say choose, defer, or escalate.”
3. Hold 60 seconds of silent reading of the question, evidence labels, and constraints.
4. Give the decision owner 60 seconds to state why the decision is needed now, the authority boundary, and the cost of no decision. No solution advocacy in this minute.
5. Give everyone 60 seconds privately to write: `current lean`, `one thing the architecture must preserve`, and `largest uncertainty`. Reveal nothing until the next phase.

**Output**: a common frame plus individual starting positions that have not yet been socially averaged.

**Guardrail**: no slide deck, biographies, status updates, icebreaker, or agenda narration beyond the decision contract.

### 03:00–10:00 — Transformed response pass

**Facilitator action**

1. Give the context proposer up to 75 seconds to state the current proposition, strongest evidence, and knowingly accepted tradeoff.
2. Route one pass through every other participant. Each gets at most 45 seconds and must use this syntax:
   - `Retain:` one premise, constraint, or benefit from the preceding proposition;
   - `Change:` one assumption, scale, direction, option, or criterion;
   - `Because:` evidence, or an explicitly labeled assumption/unknown;
   - `Consequence:` what changes in the option or decision test.
3. The recorder captures only the delta, not a transcript.
4. If time remains, route targeted clarification across different voices. No person or side receives a fourth uninterrupted exchange in one thread.

**Interruption lines**

- “That repeats the prior point. What changes?”
- “Is that evidence, an assumption, or an unknown?”
- “Name the consequence for the option or criterion.”
- “This thread now passes to another affected voice.”

**Output**: a recognizable but materially changed proposition and a trace of who altered what.

**Guardrail**: “I agree,” paraphrase, and unrelated prepared speeches do not count as responses.

### 10:00–13:00 — One provisional alignment checkpoint

**Facilitator action**

1. From 10:00–10:40, everyone silently writes: `I can support [option] if [conditions], accepting [principal tradeoff].`
2. From 10:40–11:40, reveal the statements together. The recorder clusters them without debate.
3. From 11:40–12:00, mark at most one provisional exact alignment. A match requires the same option, materially equivalent conditions, and the same principal accepted tradeoff. Similar wording is allowed; different conditions are not merged.
4. From 12:00–13:00, keep silence. Everyone writes one consequence or falsifier for any marked alignment. If there is no exact match, everyone writes the most consequential remaining difference.

**Facilitator line when needed**: “These support the same option but are not exact alignment because the conditions differ.”

**Output**: one precisely bounded provisional agreement, or an explicit record that none exists yet.

**Guardrail**: no vote, advocacy, applause, or discussion during this phase. Absence of provisional alignment is not failure.

### 13:00–21:00 — Expose decision boundaries before choosing

**Facilitator action**

1. Keep all options if there are two or three. If there are four, the decision owner may exclude one only by citing a violated hard constraint; the recorder preserves the exclusion reason.
2. For every remaining option, participants add four short entries in parallel:
   - `I would support this if …`
   - `I would reject this if …`
   - `Evidence that would flip my current view is …`
   - `The tradeoff we must knowingly accept is …`
3. Do not rebut while the fields are being populated.
4. In the last two minutes, the facilitator reads the closest pairs of support conditions that still differ and names the exact boundary. Do not average the conditions into compromise language.

**Output**: explicit support and rejection thresholds, flip evidence, and accepted-tradeoff candidates for each viable option.

**Guardrail**: this phase delays the call only until its boundaries are visible. It cannot be extended beyond minute 21.

### 21:00–27:00 — Break the leader and recover in place

**Facilitator action**

1. The challenge lead gives a maximum 60-second failure case against the current leading option. It must be plausible for this architecture and grounded in available evidence or explicitly labeled assumptions. Examples of relevant categories include dependency failure, migration rollback, security boundary, data integrity, operability, latency, cost, or ownership loss; use only what fits.
2. The option’s strongest advocate gets 60 seconds to identify which assumption, constraint interpretation, or mechanism failed. Defense without diagnosis is stopped.
3. Participants spend two silent minutes revising the option, criterion, recovery plan, or status. The recorder preserves the prior text using revision history or strikethrough.
4. The facilitator reads the before state, the break, the revision, and the residual risk. The decision owner confirms whether the leading option changed.
5. If genuine late information or a process disruption has already occurred, use it instead of inventing a scenario.

**Interruption lines**

- “Record what broke before solving it.”
- “Which assumption failed?”
- “What changes because of this?”
- “What residual risk remains after recovery?”

**Output**: a visible disruption log and a revised, more credible option or criterion set.

**Guardrail**: no staged accident, personal ambush, blame, deletion of old state, or restart with a new document.

### 27:00–35:00 — Dense parallel analysis on one spine

**Facilitator action**

1. Assign each participant one relevant architecture lane. Candidate lanes are reliability, security/privacy, data/integration, performance, cost, migration/reversibility, delivery, and operability/ownership. Combine or omit lanes to fit 5–7 people and the actual decision.
2. From 27:00–31:00, work in parallel inside the same record. Every entry is tagged `[E] evidence`, `[A] assumption`, or `[U] unknown` and lands under this fixed sequence:
   1. constraint;
   2. tradeoff;
   3. failure mode;
   4. recovery or reversibility;
   5. operating owner.
3. From 31:00–34:00, the facilitator walks the sequence in that order. Participants may correct evidence, expose a contradiction, or name an owner; they may not deliver new speeches.
4. From 34:00–35:00, the recorder drafts one candidate decision statement from the shared spine.

**Output**: multi-dimensional analysis that remains reconstructable as one decision rationale.

**Guardrail**: no independent breakout artifacts, untagged claims, or analysis lane that cannot change the option, condition, tradeoff, recovery, or ownership.

### 35:00–39:00 — Compressed role-reversed replay

**Facilitator action**

1. The participant whose opening position most strongly opposed the candidate gets 60 seconds to make its strongest current case using `retain / change / because / consequence`.
2. The initial context proposer gets 60 seconds to state the candidate’s strongest remaining rejection condition, using the same syntax. No rebuttal follows.
3. The decision owner gets 60 seconds to name what materially changed between the opening proposition and the candidate decision.
4. Everyone gets 60 silent seconds to correct factual errors in the candidate statement.

**Output**: evidence that the positions are understood across roles, plus a shorter and updated version of the opening proposition.

**Guardrail**: no impersonation, scoring of rhetorical performance, new presentation, or replay as long as the opening pass.

### 39:00–43:00 — Record the decision status

**Facilitator action**

1. The decision owner states `CHOOSE`, `DEFER`, or `ESCALATE` and fills the corresponding outcome contract.
2. The recorder completes rationale, conditions, accepted tradeoffs, dissent or watchpoints, owner, first action and date, and review or reversal trigger.
3. Allow factual corrections only in the final minute. A genuinely new safety, legal, or hard-constraint fact changes the status to a bounded defer or escalation; it does not reopen free-form debate.

**Output**: a complete architecture decision status by minute 43.

**Guardrail**: the owner does not claim consensus unless the exact alignment definition is actually met. Decision authority and participant agreement remain separate facts.

### 43:00–45:00 — Quiet, differentiated close

**Facilitator action**

1. Hold 60 seconds of silent reading of the completed record.
2. Each participant adds one short line: `I own [action] by [date]` or `I will watch [named signal/risk]`. Distinct lines are expected.
3. The facilitator verifies that status, owner, date, and trigger are present and says only: “The decision and follow-through are recorded.” End on time.

**Output**: a shared close with differentiated responsibilities and retained dissent where relevant.

**Guardrail**: no forced declaration of support, praise round, applause cue, extended recap, or post-decision victory speech.

## Facilitator quick-reference rules

- A response is valid only if it changes something relevant.
- Evidence, assumptions, and unknowns stay visibly different.
- A fourth uninterrupted exchange from the same person or side is rerouted.
- Same option with different conditions is not exact alignment.
- Every silence has a stated task.
- Conditions are recorded before the choice.
- A break changes the visible record; it is never cosmetically smoothed over.
- Parallel analysis uses one field sequence.
- Critic and proposer exchange epistemic responsibilities before the call.
- The owner decides; the group is not required to perform unanimity.
- The protocol ends with an artifact and owners, not ceremony.

## Rejection tests for shallow imitation

1. **Theme test:** If the source could be guessed from meeting music, movement, vocabulary, room staging, or phase names, reject it.
2. **Function test:** If removing an activity changes neither evidence, timing, relationship, decision balance, nor the record, remove it.
3. **Silence test:** If a pause has no assigned read/write/inspection task, it is decorative; remove or assign a task.
4. **Decision test:** If “rare agreement” prevents an accountable owner from choosing, deferring, or escalating, the translation has failed.
5. **Recovery test:** If the protocol hides the pre-break state, blames a person, or injects fake chaos, the translation has failed.
6. **Spine test:** If parallel work cannot be synthesized through one fixed record sequence, it is merely fragmented activity.
7. **Role-reversal test:** If participants act, impersonate, or debate for points rather than accurately carry the opposite position’s strongest evidence, reject it.
8. **Closure test:** If quietness leaves outcome, ownership, or deadline ambiguous, it is not restrained closure; it is incompleteness.
9. **Originality test:** If timings, counts, labels, sounds, or gestures are copied or mechanically scaled from the source, reject it.
10. **Authority test:** If equal airtime is used to conceal who actually owns the architecture decision, reject it.

## Unknowns and confidence

### Unknowns that must remain unknown

- The packet does not establish facial expression, effort quality beyond the described events, camera or audience perspective, exact musical tempo, emotional intention, audience interpretation, or whether other performances vary.
- The packet does not establish why the choreographer selected the specific durations, only that the rehearsal note preserves several relationships. The meeting timings above are target-generated operational choices, not claimed equivalents.
- The architecture topic, organization, authority model, evidence quality, conflict level, accessibility needs, and risk classification are unspecified. They must be instantiated at setup.
- A 45-minute meeting may be insufficient for a high-blast-radius irreversible choice with missing evidence. In that case the honest output is a bounded defer or escalation, not fabricated certainty.

- **High confidence:** reciprocal transformation, bounded dominance, rare exact convergence, visible in-sequence recovery, a stable common direction under density, role-reversed compression, and functional restraint are directly repeated or named in the packet and rehearsal note.
