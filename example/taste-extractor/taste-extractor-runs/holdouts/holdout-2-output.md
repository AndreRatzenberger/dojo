# Taste profile — pocket field-guide family translated to a disruption-line IVR

## Source and access

- Source artifacts: three core pocket guides and one documented limited festival edition, as described in the supplied evidence packet.
- Source medium: printed, thread-sewn field guides; physical object, editorial system, and information-design system.
- Downstream target: interactive voice response system for a regional public-transport disruption line.
- Required target capabilities: hear current disruption summaries; check a route; repeat the most recent information; hear information more slowly; reach a human during staffed hours.
- Inspected: the complete written evidence packet, including core-series rules, the limited-edition variant, and the editor's explicit acceptance criteria.
- Unavailable: the physical books, images of pages or covers, actual paper/print samples, recordings of anyone using the books, and any further artifacts. For the target, no caller research, accessibility requirements, languages, staffed hours, route-data schema, data-freshness policy, speech-recognition capability, keypad constraints, voice guidelines, or failure statistics were supplied.
- Fidelity boundary: transfer the information relationships, pacing, hierarchy, uncertainty behavior, and restraint. Do not transfer field-guide subject matter, printed-object metaphors, nature motifs, or page-turn behavior.

## Taste thesis

A calm, repeatable information grammar pairs a terse overview with opt-in detail, exposes uncertainty instead of smoothing it away, and provides redundant but restrained paths through dense material, with brief resets and plain language keeping the system trustworthy.

## Recurrence and confidence model

- **High confidence:** a behavior recurs across all three core guides and is also named in the editor's refusal criteria. This applies to paired overview/detail, explicit uncertainty, compact headings, restrained redundant navigation, brief sparse pauses, and common-language-first lookup.
- **Medium-high confidence:** a behavior recurs across all three core guides but is not separately named by the editor. This applies to the stable normal-spread grammar, generous outer space with denser indexed detail, and independent-but-linked modules.
- **Medium confidence:** a recurring surface choice supports a plausible portable relationship, but the packet does not identify it as indispensable. This applies to the single saturated accent per volume and the cover's extreme restraint.
- **High confidence as a non-invariant:** foil, fluorescent band, and fold-out appear once, are absent from the other three guides, and are explicitly called “event-only signals, not series rules.” Their recurrence count is one and the production note excludes them from the core taste contract.
- **High confidence as adaptable:** exact accent colors, page counts, and subjects are explicitly allowed to change. Exact print dimensions, word counts, and physical construction are observed source facts, not automatic target-medium requirements.

## Transfer layers

### Source content — do not transfer

- Any guide subjects, identification examples, technical names, location/index codes, drawings, numbered specimens, or exact phrases from the books.
- The phrase “compare before deciding” as required IVR copy. Preserve its function, not its wording.
- The books' exact dimensions, page counts, entry word counts, physical construction, or any volume identity.
- The festival's event identity or any implied subject matter.

### Surface manifestations — adapt deliberately or discard

- Warm uncoated paper, matte charcoal fields, lowercase typography, line drawings, numbered callouts, gutter indexes, fore-edge marks, accent-color stock, and solid/hollow markers are print manifestations. None should be imitated literally in audio.
- The split spread is not a reason to describe “left,” “right,” “pages,” or “turning.” Its job becomes overview/detail sequencing.
- The exact two-to-four-word heading and 70–110-word entry are source-specific measurements. The transferable rule is compact labeling plus bounded content.
- A short pause can carry the sparse-section-opening function, but silence must remain brief enough not to sound like a dropped call.
- Speech plus keypad can carry restrained navigation redundancy. Audible controls must remain usable without speech recognition.
- A confirmation phrase can carry the solid-callout function, and explicit uncertainty language can carry the hollow-callout function. A sonic marker may supplement but never replace the words.
- Foil, fluorescent wrapping, and fold-out panorama have no core IVR analogue and should not inspire shimmer effects, fanfares, soundscapes, or an extended special-event narration.

### Portable principles — preserve

1. A brief overview and a deeper detail layer are independently understandable and precisely linked.
2. A stable repeated grammar makes a collection of dense items easy to navigate.
3. Density increases only after the caller asks for it.
4. Uncertainty is explicit, differentiated from confirmed information, and paired with useful discriminators or a next verification step.
5. Navigation is redundant enough to be robust and restrained enough to remain legible.
6. Context changes use short, functional resets rather than decorative transitions.
7. Common language leads; official or technical identifiers remain available secondarily.
8. Exceptional-event emphasis may vary, but the core operating grammar remains intact.

## Signature principles

1. **Overview beside detail.** Give callers the smallest useful answer first, then make a deeper answer immediately available. Preserve context across the handoff.
2. **Stable modular grammar.** Every disruption item and route result follows a familiar sequence. Callers should learn the system after hearing one item.
3. **Calm outside, dense inside.** Entry points are sparse; operational density appears only after selection. Long, undifferentiated announcements are a failure.
4. **Actionable uncertainty.** State uncertainty in ordinary words and name what would resolve it or what the caller can do safely now.
5. **Quiet redundancy.** Offer more than one dependable way to navigate, but keep a single consistent vocabulary and control map.
6. **Brief reset, rapid resumption.** Mark a new context, pause briefly, then return to the normal information grammar.
7. **Human terms first.** Familiar route, destination, stop, direction, and impact language precede technical labels and codes.

## Productive tensions

- Compact, not cryptic.
- Dense, not crowded.
- Calm, not sluggish.
- Repetitive in structure, not repetitive in content.
- Redundant, not noisy.
- Honest about uncertainty, not evasive.
- Modular, not fragmented.
- Precise, not specialist-first.
- Sparse at transitions, not silent enough to feel broken.
- Distinctive in hierarchy, not decorated with audio effects.
- Self-service capable, not a barrier to human help.

## Invariants

- The disruption overview and route-specific detail must both be useful without requiring the other, and the transition between them must restate enough context to prevent ambiguity.
- Each disruption or route result must begin with a compact, plain-language orientation before denser detail.
- The system must explicitly distinguish confirmed information from unconfirmed, partial, or conflicting information in words.
- Uncertainty must be followed by useful resolution information: the key distinction, the next expected confirmation point, or the safest available action, depending on the data supplied.
- Repeat and slower playback must be reachable at the point of need and operate on the last meaningful content block rather than restarting the entire call.
- Navigation labels and controls must remain consistent throughout the call. If both speech and keypad are available, they must lead to equivalent outcomes.
- A human-transfer path must be plainly announced and functional during staffed hours; it must not be hidden behind repeated self-service failures.
- Context transitions must remain short and functional, followed by an immediate return to the normal information grammar.
- Common-language route and disruption terms must come before formal codes or operator terminology.
- A special-event mode, if one exists, must retain the normal route checks, controls, uncertainty behavior, and human-access path.

## Degrees of freedom

- Human-recorded voice versus text-to-speech, voice identity, accent, and emotional warmth, subject to caller testing and accessibility needs.
- Exact wording, prompt length, pause duration, speaking rate, and slower-mode rate. These require listening tests; the print packet does not establish audio measurements.
- Exact keypad numbers and speech keywords. The mapping may vary, but it must be fixed within the system, announced economically, and documented.
- Whether the first response begins with a systemwide status sentence or the main choices, depending on disruption volume and emergency policy. It must not become a long forced briefing.
- Route identification by spoken route name, keypad entry, stop, destination, or another locally valid method. Common-language entry remains primary.
- Number of detail layers, provided the first layer stays concise and later layers do not strand the caller.
- Whether a short neutral tone supports a transition or confirmation. It is optional, sparse, and never the sole signal.
- Exact uncertainty vocabulary, governed by the transport operator's data states and legal requirements. It must remain explicit and useful.
- Languages and language-selection behavior, based on caller needs. Every language version must preserve the same hierarchy and controls rather than acting as a reduced feature set.
- A temporary high-severity preface or priority item, provided it is short, time-bounded, and leaves the core grammar intact.

## IVR production rules

### Global behavior

- Maintain a persistent call context: selected route, direction or destination when known, current content block, playback mode, and whether the caller has requested an agent.
- Announce data freshness when the operational feed supplies it and when freshness affects the usefulness of “current.” Do not invent a timestamp or imply live status without data support.
- Use one short sentence per major fact. Put the most consequential impact before cause or background.
- Introduce the repeat and slower-playback controls early, then make them valid after every meaningful information block. Do not reread the full controls after every block unless the caller asks for help.
- “Repeat” repeats the last meaningful summary or detail block with its context label. It does not restart the greeting.
- “Slower” re-renders the same block using shorter phrases and more separation between facts. It must not merely increase volume or distort a recording.
- Every tone or pause is supplementary. All state, confidence, and choices are expressed in speech.
- Speech-recognition failure must not remove keypad access. If speech input is unavailable, the keypad path still provides all core functions.

### Entry and main choices

- Keep the greeting functional: identify the disruption line, state any essential freshness limitation, and move directly to choices.
- Present three primary jobs in ordinary language: hear current disruptions; check a route; speak to a person. Announce staffed-hours qualification with the human option without making the caller attempt transfer to discover it.
- Mention repeat and slower playback once in a compact global-help sentence. The implementation team may assign the exact keys; those keys must remain constant.
- Do not force every caller through all current disruptions before allowing a route check or human transfer.

### Current disruption summary

- Start with a one-sentence overview that is useful by itself, including “no current disruptions” when that is the supported state.
- Present each disruption as its own block using the stable order: compact label; affected route, direction, or place; main impact; relevant time/freshness; confidence state; immediate next action if supplied.
- After each block, offer only locally relevant actions: detail, next disruption, repeat, slower, back, or human. Avoid replaying the complete main menu.
- A caller who requests detail hears the context label again, then optional denser information. Returning from detail resumes at the correct position in the summary.

### Route check

- Ask first for the familiar route name or number. Ask for direction, destination, stop, or operator only when needed to resolve ambiguity.
- When several routes plausibly match, name the smallest useful set of distinctions and let the caller choose; do not guess silently.
- State a compact route overview first. Then offer affected stops or segment, expected impact, timing, alternatives, cause/background, and confidence as available detail.
- If no disruption is found, say exactly what the data supports. Do not convert “no record found” into “service is definitely normal.” Offer another route, current disruptions, repeat, slower playback, or human help.

### Uncertainty behavior

- Map actual backend states to reviewed spoken labels for confirmed, unconfirmed, partial, conflicting, stale, and unavailable data. Do not collapse them into one generic warning.
- For uncertain information, order the block as: what is known; what is not confirmed; the two most useful distinctions when two plausible states must be compared, or the next expected update/verification route when comparison is not possible; a safe action if the operator supplies one.
- Never infer certainty from the absence of an alert. Never hide a stale feed behind calm delivery.
- When uncertainty affects a transfer decision or safety, correctness and operator policy override brevity and stylistic restraint.

### Human handoff and recovery

- During staffed hours, the announced human option initiates transfer directly after any legally or operationally required short notice.
- Outside staffed hours, state that a person is unavailable, announce the next staffed period only if supplied by operations, and return to useful self-service choices. Do not simulate a transfer.
- On no input or no match, preserve the current context, give one compact correction, and offer keypad entry. Repeated failure should expose human help during staffed hours rather than looping indefinitely.
- On data-feed failure, say that current status cannot be confirmed, retain route-check and human choices where they still function, and avoid presenting cached data as current unless its age is spoken.
- On transfer failure, state the failure plainly and return the caller to the last useful context with self-service and retry options.

## Production sequence

1. Define the operational data states, freshness states, staffed-hours source, transfer behavior, route identifiers, and failure states before writing polished prompts.
2. Create the single repeated disruption schema: label, affected service/place, impact, time/freshness, confidence, next action, and optional detail.
3. Design a sparse entry path with direct access to current disruptions, route check, and a human, plus fixed repeat and slower-playback controls.
4. Script overview and detail as independently understandable blocks. Specify exactly which context fields are repeated when entering detail.
5. Write common-language route collection and disambiguation before technical-code handling.
6. Write separate confirmed, uncertain, stale, unavailable, and no-record-found responses. For uncertain states, identify the useful discriminators or next verification step the data can genuinely support.
7. Add recovery paths for no input, no match, data failure, out-of-hours transfer, and transfer failure without losing call context.
8. Implement normal and slower renderings from the same semantic content so they cannot drift in meaning.
9. Test both speech and keypad paths where speech is supported, including repeat and slower playback after every content state and direct human transfer during staffed hours.
10. Listen with representative callers and revise wording, pauses, rate, and menu load. Preserve the invariants while changing target-specific measurements.
11. Run the yes/no validation checks below against normal, uncertain, empty, error, severe-event, staffed, and unstaffed scenarios before release.

## Ready-to-use downstream prompt

Design and implement the prompt architecture and state behavior for a regional public-transport disruption-line IVR. The IVR must let callers hear current disruption summaries, check a route, repeat the last meaningful information block, hear that block more slowly, and reach a human during staffed hours.

Use this taste contract:

- Lead with a compact plain-language overview; make denser detail opt-in. Overview and detail must each make sense alone and must be linked by a repeated route, direction/place, and impact context.
- Give every disruption and route result the same grammar: short label; affected service/place; main impact; time or data freshness when available; explicit confidence state; immediate next action; optional detail.
- Keep entry prompts sparse and fact blocks short. Put impact before cause. Use a brief spoken context reset and a short pause between layers, then resume the normal grammar.
- State uncertainty explicitly in words. Say what is known, what is not confirmed, and the most useful distinctions, next expected update, or safe action that the actual data supports. Never make a tone the only confidence signal and never turn “no record found” into a guarantee of normal service.
- Use common route, destination, and stop language before official codes. Ask for disambiguating details only when necessary.
- Keep navigation redundant but restrained. Where speech input is supported, offer an equivalent fixed keypad path. Repeat, slower playback, back, and human help must behave consistently and preserve call context.
- During staffed hours, human transfer is a direct, plainly announced option, not a reward after failed automation. Outside staffed hours, state unavailability and return to useful self-service. Handle failed transfer and data-feed failure explicitly.
- Implement slower playback as the same semantic block in shorter phrases with more spacing, not as louder or distorted audio. Repeat only the last meaningful block with its context.
- An exceptional severe-event preface may be more prominent, but it must be brief and then return to the unchanged menu, information grammar, controls, uncertainty behavior, and human path.

Do not imitate the printed source. No field-guide language, drawings, numbered specimens, page or gutter metaphors, nature motifs, paper/foil references, page-turn effects, ambient station scenes, fanfares, route-specific characters, or decorative sound palette. Accessibility, correctness, emergency policy, and telephony reliability outrank stylistic fidelity.

Deliver: a call-state map; prompt copy for normal, empty, uncertain, stale, unavailable, no-input, no-match, out-of-hours, data-failure, transfer-failure, and severe-event states; the fixed speech/keypad control map; semantic templates for overview, detail, repeat, and slower playback; and a test matrix covering every required capability. Mark any operational field not supplied by the transport operator as unresolved rather than inventing it.

## Rejection tests for shallow imitation

- Reject if the proposal uses page, spread, index, gutter, margin, callout, field-guide, specimen, or page-turn language in caller-facing prompts.
- Reject if print colors, materials, foil, fluorescent accents, drawings, or fold-outs have merely been converted into earcons, sound effects, voice characters, music, or scenic audio.
- Reject if “overview/detail” is represented as two long consecutive narrations with no caller choice or independent usefulness.
- Reject if uncertainty is expressed only through hedging, a generic disclaimer, or a tone without stating what is unknown and what resolves it.
- Reject if every route or disruption has a bespoke prompt structure; novelty here destroys the recurring grammar.
- Reject if navigation redundancy becomes a long list of synonyms, controls, and reminders after every utterance.
- Reject if technical identifiers are required before a caller can ask about a familiar route, stop, or destination.
- Reject if an event or emergency mode replaces the normal controls or strands callers in a special narration.
- Reject if “calm” becomes slow, emotionally flat delivery that delays urgent impact information.
- Reject if the automation hides human access, loops on recognition failure, or claims certainty the data does not support.
