# Taste profile — everyday tea tin packaging system

## Source and access

- **Source artifacts:** Structured observations of three related tea tins and a production note describing a gift edition.
- **Source medium:** Packaging system; cylindrical physical containers with paper labels.
- **Downstream target:** A fourth everyday tea tin to be designed by another agent.
- **Inspected:** The supplied observations covering shared form, label material, front-label typography, the variable ink-wash device, exterior information, under-lid instructions, and the gift edition's seasonal additions.
- **Unavailable:** Direct views or physical inspection; exact dimensions and dielines; all sides and seams; tin material and finish; paper specification; typeface identity and full typographic system; color values; print processes; exact wash shapes and existing hues; placement and styling of origin, harvest month, and weight; under-lid layout; regulatory requirements; production tolerances.
- **Constraints and exclusions:** Extract the reusable system and hand it off; do not design the fourth tin. Treat gold foil and the wide ribbon as seasonal gift-edition additions, not everyday system rules. Do not transfer tea-specific content or copy an existing wash.
- **Desired fidelity:** Preserve the collection's relationships, hierarchy, and boundaries while creating an original fourth member rather than a replica of any one source tin.

## Taste thesis

A quiet, collectible packaging system holds a fixed slender, matte, typographically restrained base while giving each everyday tea one bounded organic color gesture and revealing functional detail only when the package is opened.

## Transfer layers

### Source content — do not transfer

- Existing tea names, origins, harvest months, weights, and brewing copy.
- Any exact wash contour, placement, muted hue, edge, bloom, or internal texture from an existing tin.
- Any source-specific production artwork, illustration, or identifier not described as a shared system rule.
- Gold foil and ribbon from the seasonal gift edition.

### Surface manifestations — adapt deliberately

- The tall, narrow cylinder and matte ivory paper label are repeated family components. For this same-medium collection extension, retain them using the actual approved dieline, stock, and tolerances when available.
- The small black serif tea name aligned low on the front is a repeated compositional rule. Use the established family type asset if supplied; do not guess an exact typeface from the description.
- The single muted vertical wash is the SKU-expression device. Generate a fresh contour and choose a hue distinct from the existing set while preserving its bounded role.
- Under-lid brewing instructions are part of the information sequence, not an invitation to hide legally or operationally necessary content.

### Portable principles — preserve

- Stable family structure with one tightly bounded variable.
- Restraint as hierarchy: few elements, ample quiet field, no ornamental competition.
- Organic expression disciplined by margins and legibility.
- Progressive disclosure tied to the use sequence.
- Explicit separation between everyday grammar and seasonal ceremony.

## Signature principles

1. **Family first, variation second.** The shared silhouette, label ground, and naming behavior should establish membership before the unique wash differentiates the tea.
2. **Quiet materials and typography carry confidence.** Matte ivory, black serif type, small scale, and low placement avoid shouting.
3. **Permit one disciplined irregularity.** The wash may be organic, but its count, orientation, saturation family, edge clearance, and separation from text are controlled.
4. **Reveal information when it becomes useful.** The outside identifies the tea; opening the lid reveals preparation guidance.
5. **Keep special editions exceptional.** Foil and ribbon express a seasonal gift condition and must not leak into the everyday baseline.

## Productive tensions

- Consistent, not cloned.
- Organic, not uncontrolled.
- Quiet, not anonymous.
- Sparse, not functionally incomplete.
- Tactile, not precious.
- Collectible, not decoratively busy.
- Progressively disclosed, not difficult to use.

## Invariants

- Use the established tall, narrow cylindrical package family.
- Use a matte ivory paper label.
- Place one small black serif tea name low on the front.
- Use exactly one irregular vertical ink wash in a muted color.
- Keep the wash short of both label edges.
- Keep all text out of the wash; the wash must not become a text background.
- Show only tea name, origin, harvest month, and weight on the everyday exterior, subject to verified mandatory-content requirements.
- Put brewing instructions beneath the lid.
- Do not add gold foil or a wide ribbon to the everyday tin.

## Degrees of freedom

- The new tea's factual name, origin, harvest month, weight, and brewing instructions.
- A new muted wash hue, provided it is checked against the other three and remains distinct without becoming bright or loud.
- The wash's original contour, internal variation, width, height, and precise vertical placement within the established clear-zone constraints.
- The arrangement and styling of origin, harvest month, and weight, because the evidence establishes their presence but not their coordinates or typography; prefer existing family files if available.
- Under-lid instruction layout, within readability, production, and available-area constraints.
- Print method, paper weight, coating specification, adhesives, seam treatment, and tolerances where the approved production system does not already fix them.
- Necessary accessibility, manufacturing, or regulatory adaptations; document any exception to the observed exterior information budget.

## Anti-patterns and originality guardrails

- Do not copy an existing tea's name, provenance data, wording, wash contour, or wash color.
- Do not reproduce one source tin element-for-element and merely replace its tea name.
- Do not let “organic” expand into splatters, multiple marks, full-bleed color, distressed type, or generalized handmade decoration.
- Do not place any text over the wash, even if contrast appears technically sufficient.
- Do not add marketing claims, tasting notes, badges, illustrations, logos, borders, or extra exterior instructions without a functional requirement and explicit system review.
- Do not promote gift-edition foil or ribbon into the everyday family.
- Do not invent exact dimensions, color values, typeface identities, clearances, or production tolerances absent from the evidence; obtain them from approved assets or mark them for decision.
- Do not interpret restraint as tiny unreadable metadata or omission of required information. Legibility, factual correctness, and mandatory packaging content outrank stylistic fidelity.
- Preserve the relationship — fixed quiet base plus one bounded organic variable — rather than the literal content of any source member.

## Production sequence

1. Obtain the approved everyday cylinder dieline, label dimensions and stock, family type assets, production tolerances, required legal copy, and the three existing wash artworks or at least their color references.
2. Lock the invariant package form and matte ivory label before exploring SKU variation.
3. Insert the fourth tea's verified exterior data. Place the tea name small, black, serif, and low; use existing family rules for the other fields when available.
4. Establish text and edge clear zones before creating the wash.
5. Create one original irregular vertical wash in a muted hue not already used. Reject candidates that touch label edges, sit behind text, or introduce a second decorative idea.
6. Typeset verified brewing instructions beneath the lid and check readability in the actual production area.
7. Proof the assembled package for collection fit, factual accuracy, accessibility, print feasibility, seam behavior, and mandatory-content compliance.
8. Run the yes/no checks below and record any justified exception before releasing artwork.
