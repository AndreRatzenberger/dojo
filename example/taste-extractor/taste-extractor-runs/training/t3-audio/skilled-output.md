## Taste thesis

A stable shared structure supports delayed individual responses, controlled overlap, constructive recovery from collision, exchanged roles, and an ending that leaves space rather than forcing closure (P1–P6).

## Transfer layers

### Source content — do not transfer

Do not reproduce pitches, intervals, instruments, timing ratios, compositional events, or the source’s exact sequence as performance or metaphor.

### Surface manifestations — adapt deliberately

Sparse events become silent writing and bounded turns. Delayed responses become observation-before-discussion. Overlap becomes parallel causal perspectives. The collision becomes a discrepancy-handling procedure. Role exchange becomes perspective restatement. Progressive removal becomes action prioritization.

### Portable principles — preserve

- P1: stable structure with usable space
- P2: delayed, distinct responses
- P3: brief convergence followed by renewed inquiry
- P4: plurality held against a common reference
- P5: constructive recovery without reset or blame
- P6: changed perspective on recurring material
- P7: gradual narrowing with honest incompleteness

## Signature principles

1. **Anchor before interpretation (P1).** Establish the incident scope and known timeline before discussing causes.
2. **Think separately before responding collectively (P2).** Give each participant uninterrupted time to form an account.
3. **Pause after agreement (P3).** Use consensus to stabilize facts, then reopen inquiry.
4. **Keep plurality legible (P4).** Allow several contributing explanations while tying each to evidence.
5. **Convert friction into evidence (P5).** Treat disagreement as information about context, interfaces, or assumptions.
6. **Exchange perspective without transferring authority (P6).** Participants may restate another role’s constraints; the original owner confirms or corrects the restatement.
7. **Close with bounded commitments and visible unknowns (P7).** Do not imply that every cause has been resolved.

## Productive tensions

- Structured, not rigid (P1, P5)
- Spacious, not passive (P1, P2)
- Convergent, not prematurely settled (P3, P7)
- Multi-perspective, not diffuse (P4)
- Candid, not adversarial (P5)
- Empathetic, not authority-blurring (P6)
- Actionable, not artificially complete (P7)

## Invariants

- One incident scope and one shared factual timeline remain visible throughout (P1, P4).
- Every person contributes before open discussion begins (P2).
- Initial observations are not interrupted, corrected, or rebutted (P2).
- Facts, interpretations, and unknowns remain visibly distinct (P3, P4).
- Disagreement triggers clarification and evidence capture, not blame or restart (P5).
- A participant may describe another role’s constraints, but only that role’s representative validates the description (P6).
- Each action has exactly one accountable owner; no action is assigned twice or jointly owned (P7).
- Unresolved questions remain explicit at close (P7).

## Degrees of freedom

The facilitator may adapt the collaboration tool, participant role names, prompt wording, order within a round, and number of contributing factors discussed. They may shorten discussion sections to protect the closing sequence. They may not remove the individual preparation, uninterrupted first round, shared timeline, discrepancy procedure, perspective check, or explicit owner/unknown close.

## 30-minute action register

The incident lead owns factual confirmation. Domain representatives retain authority over claims about their own systems or decisions. The facilitator owns process and time only.

### Discrepancy procedure

When accounts collide, the facilitator asks:

1. “Which parts are directly observed?”
2. “Which assumption makes these accounts differ?”
3. “What evidence would distinguish them?”
4. “Who can obtain that evidence, and by when?”

If the fourth answer creates work, it must be registered during 23:00–27:00 with exactly one owner. Otherwise it remains an unresolved question, not an implied action.

## Anti-patterns and originality guardrails

Reject the meeting design if it:

- uses performance, sound cues, music terminology, theatrical role-play, or decorative references to the source;
- copies the source’s exact timings or event count;
- permits open debate before everyone contributes (P2);
- mistakes brief agreement for completed analysis (P3);
- reduces the incident to one root cause without evidence (P4);
- treats disagreement as misconduct or restarts the discussion from zero (P5);
- lets perspective-taking transfer factual or decision authority (P6);
- ends with vague collective ownership, duplicate assignment, or manufactured consensus (P7).

## Ready-to-use facilitator prompt

> Facilitate a 30-minute, five-person incident retrospective using the supplied action register exactly as the meeting backbone. Maintain one visible incident scope and timeline. Separate facts, interpretations, and unknowns. Begin with individual writing, then give every participant one uninterrupted turn before discussion. After the incident lead confirms shared facts, pause briefly before causal exploration. Develop multiple evidence-linked contributing conditions rather than forcing one root cause. When accounts conflict, record both, identify the differing assumption or evidence, define what could resolve the discrepancy, and continue without blame.
>
> During the perspective pass, ask each participant to restate another role’s constraint; only the represented role may confirm or correct that account. In the action phase, accept no more than three actions. Every action must have exactly one accountable owner, an expected outcome, a due date, and a verification signal. Keep unresolved questions visible. Close with each person’s next needed signal, without forcing a final consensus statement. Protect factual accuracy, permissions, ownership, security, risk, and other high-stakes language from stylistic softening.
