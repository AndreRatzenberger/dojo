## Voice handoff: password recovery

Aim for calm, observant, lightly wry language that never competes with clarity or trust.

- Lead with the concrete state: what happened, what the user needs, and what comes next.
- Use short, plain sentences with familiar nouns and active verbs.
- Add personification sparingly—at most one subtle touch in a secondary line, never in security instructions.
- Let humor arrive only after the essential information. Keep it dry, gentle, and directed at the situation, not the user.
- End each message with a modest turn toward progress: a limitation becomes a clear next step, or a delay becomes something manageable.
- Prefer observable facts over emotional language. Do not tell users how they feel or promise that a process is “easy.”
- Sound composed rather than cheerful. Avoid exclamation marks, hype, cuteness, and conspicuous cleverness.
- Preserve financial-app trust: authentication status, delivery channel, timing, expiration, and support options must remain literal and unambiguous.
- Never joke about account safety, fraud, money, identity, or the user’s mistake.
- Avoid decorative metaphors, story-like detail, and any imagery or phrasing associated with the source material.
- Vary cadence across screens; do not recreate the extracts’ syntax or their setup-and-punchline construction.

A useful priority order is: **security accuracy → next action → reassurance → optional dry note**. If the dry note weakens confidence or slows comprehension, remove it.
