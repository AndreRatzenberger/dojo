## Taste thesis

An action-first public-information language concentrates attention on a reachable destination, surrounds it with visual breathing room, and states entry and operational conditions plainly (`P1–P6`).

## Transfer layers

### Source content — do not reproduce as style

Do not recreate:

- The exact bus-shelter composition
- The source map geometry or lower-right endpoint placement
- The pointing-hand shot
- The exact arrangement of four people or the row of bottles
- The exact three-rectangle stack
- A sequence of three poster panels that reconstructs the sampled frames as a storyboard

The transcript’s identification, hours, and accessibility information may be retained as required operational content, subject to municipal verification; it is not a decorative style motif.

### Surface manifestations — adapt deliberately

- Orange may remain an action or destination color (`P2`, `P4`), but its exact source value is unknown.
- Blue may remain a route or navigational color (`P2`), but must not be the only route cue.
- Pale open fields may create separation and calm (`P3`).
- Modular blocks may organize changing information (`P4`, `P6`) without copying their source count or proportions.
- Map and human-support imagery may be used for their navigational and practical functions (`P2`), not as shot recreation.

### Portable principles — preserve

1. **Action before atmosphere** (`P1`)
2. **One unmistakable destination** (`P2`)
3. **Urgency with breathing room** (`P3`)
4. **Distance-readable modular signaling** (`P4`)
5. **Barrier removal beside the apparent barrier** (`P5`)
6. **Operational facts as updateable modules** (`P6`)

## Productive tensions

- Urgent, not alarming (`P1`, `P3`)
- Sparse, not incomplete (`P3`, `P6`)
- Directional, not geographically overloaded (`P2`)
- Modular, not mechanically imitative (`P4`)
- Conditional, not exclusionary (`P5`)
- Operational, not bureaucratic (`P5`, `P6`)

## Invariants

- The primary directive must be understandable before map detail (`P1`).
- The cooling-center destination must be labeled by text and a non-color cue, not color alone (`P2`).
- The access statement must explicitly say that entry is available without identification (`P5`).
- Today’s hours and accessible-route information must have distinct labeled modules (`P6`).
- Open space must survive content expansion; optional material is removed before the primary hierarchy is compressed (`P3`).
- Accessibility, correctness, and operational clarity always outrank stylistic fidelity.

Executable access check: **Does the poster literally state that a person can enter without identification? Yes/No.**

## Degrees of freedom

The producer may choose:

- Typeface family, because source typography is unknown
- Exact grid dimensions and print sizes
- Exact orange, blue, neutral, and text values after contrast testing
- Photography, illustration, icons, or a map-only treatment
- Portrait, landscape, or narrow-panel reflow
- Number and shape of modules
- Whether route information is a drawn path, directional diagram, or labeled accessible-route panel
- Additional validated contact or language-access information

These choices may not weaken `P1–P6` or the invariants.

# Static poster implementation handoff

All dimensions and sizes below are target-medium production decisions, not claims about the source.

## Function-based translation

| Principle | Source manifestation | Static-poster decision | Literal copy to avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| `P1` Action before atmosphere | Imperative transcript opening | Put “Find the nearest open cooling center before the afternoon peak” or municipally approved equivalent in the first reading zone. | Reconstructing the opening as a video caption or timeline |
| `P2` One destination | Thick route converging on orange circle | Use a simplified map with a dominant route, labeled destination, and redundant endpoint symbol. | Copying the source route bend, endpoint coordinates, or pointing hand |
| `P3` Breathing room | Empty pale upper field | Reserve uncluttered space around the directive and destination; keep secondary notices out of this field. | Reproducing the sky band or seated-group framing |
| `P4` Modular signaling | Vertical orange rectangle stack | Use a flexible family of high-visibility modules for action, hours, and access. | Using exactly three orange rectangles in the same arrangement |
| `P5` Barrier removal | Identification request immediately followed by assurance | Place the request and assurance together, with the assurance at equal or greater emphasis. | Hiding “without identification” in a disclaimer |
| `P6` Updateable operations | Direction to check today’s hours and accessible-route information | Create independently replaceable hours and accessibility modules. | Treating time-sensitive facts as decorative supporting copy |

## Poster grid

Use a six-column portrait master with a generous outer margin and three functional zones:

1. **Action and destination zone — approximately top quarter**
   - Directive across four to six columns
   - Center name or destination directly beneath
   - Optional verified “open now” status only when the data can be maintained

2. **Navigation zone — approximately middle two-fifths**
   - Simplified local map
   - “You are here,” destination, route, and street/transit anchors
   - Route and endpoint dominate incidental geography

3. **Access and operations zone — remaining lower area**
   - Identification statement
   - Today’s hours
   - Accessible-route information
   - Optional validated contact or language-access details

Use one empty grid column, or equivalent negative space, around the principal directive wherever the format permits (`P3`). Do not fill unused space with generic municipal messaging.

## Information hierarchy

1. **Primary action:** Find the nearest open cooling center before the afternoon peak (`P1`).
2. **Destination:** Verified center name and address (`P2`).
3. **Navigation:** Map, “You are here,” route, and destination label (`P2`).
4. **Access assurance:** Entry remains available without identification (`P5`).
5. **Today’s hours:** Date-specific opening and closing times (`P6`).
6. **Accessible route:** Step-free entrance, accessible stop/parking information, or a literal statement that details require confirmation (`P6`).
7. **Optional support:** Verified contact and language-access channels.

Never allow a logo, campaign slogan, photograph, or decorative headline to outrank levels 1–4.

## Type roles

Because `typography` is unknown, use an original, highly legible sans-serif system rather than claiming source fidelity.

- **Directive:** Largest, boldest role; short line length; sentence case
- **Destination:** Large bold role, visually joined to the map endpoint
- **Module heading:** Compact bold role for “ENTRY,” “TODAY’S HOURS,” and “ACCESSIBLE ROUTE”
- **Operational text:** Plain, high-contrast body role with generous spacing
- **Map label:** Bold enough to remain readable over simplified geography
- **Update metadata:** Smaller supporting role for “Updated [date/time],” never used for access conditions

Do not use condensed or decorative faces for operational copy. Do not rely on weight alone to distinguish critical labels; combine weight with spacing, position, or shape.

## Color roles

- **Action/destination color:** Orange role derived from `FRAME-00:03` and `FRAME-00:11`; use for the destination marker and limited high-priority emphasis.
- **Route/information color:** Blue role derived from `FRAME-00:11`; use for route lines and navigational information.
- **Quiet field:** White or pale neutral role derived from `FRAME-00:03` and `FRAME-00:19`; use for breathing room and module separation.
- **Text:** Near-black or another verified high-contrast dark.
- **Redundancy:** Every color-coded item also receives a text label, icon, line pattern, border, or shape distinction.

Exact color values must be selected through print and contrast testing; they cannot be extracted from the descriptions.

## Map and destination treatment

- Use only verified municipal map data.
- Remove streets and landmarks that do not help someone reach the center.
- Mark “YOU ARE HERE” with a distinct symbol and text.
- Draw the usable route with a visually dominant line.
- Mark the cooling center with a filled endpoint symbol plus the words “COOLING CENTER.”
- Connect the endpoint visually to the center name and address.
- If an accessible route differs from the shortest route, show it separately and label it “ACCESSIBLE ROUTE”; do not imply the routes are identical.
- Do not use orange versus blue as the sole distinction.
- Do not copy the sampled route bend or lower-right endpoint placement.

## Access statement

Use literal, barrier-removing language:

> Bring identification if you have it. You may enter without identification.

“Without identification” must be at least as prominent as “Bring identification.” Do not replace it with “ID optional,” “subject to policy,” or another compressed phrase that could blur entry rights. Any municipally required qualification must be verified and written literally.

## Hours module

Required fields:

- **TODAY’S HOURS**
- Calendar date
- Opening time
- Closing time
- Last verified date/time
- Clear status when hours are unavailable or awaiting confirmation

Do not print “open,” “open now,” or a closing time unless operationally verified. The module must be independently replaceable without redesigning the poster (`P6`).

## Accessibility module

Required heading:

- **ACCESSIBLE ROUTE**

Include only verified facts, such as:

- Step-free entrance location
- Accessible transit stop or parking point
- Elevator or ramp availability
- Distance or directional instruction
- A literal contact/check instruction when route details are not confirmed

Do not use a generic accessibility icon as a substitute for route information.

## Anti-patterns and originality guardrails

Reject a design that:

- Reconstructs the three sampled frames as sequential panels
- Copies the source’s exact object count, route geometry, marker position, or rectangle stack
- Turns orange blocks, a pointing hand, bottles, or pale sky into decorative source mimicry
- Uses a heat-wave photograph as atmosphere while weakening navigation
- Adds dense municipal boilerplate to the primary reading field
- Places access assurance in fine print
- Treats a wheelchair symbol as complete accessible-route information
- Uses unverified “open now,” hours, address, route, or entry claims
- Infers type, motion, sound, or voice characteristics from unavailable evidence
- Sacrifices accessibility or correctness for resemblance
