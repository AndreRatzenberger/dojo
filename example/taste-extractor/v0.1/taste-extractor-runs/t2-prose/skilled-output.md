## Taste thesis

Calm, observant prose turns ordinary hesitation into dry, humane comedy by letting precise details and sentence rhythm carry the emotion, then finds a modest foothold without pretending the larger problem has been solved.

## Transfer layers

### Source content — do not transfer

- The named character, the early-hour setting, greenhouse, vents, pipe, seedlings, compass direction, instructions, colored envelope, compost bin, tea, and the passage's sequence of events.
- Any exact or near-exact phrase, including the descriptions of waking, reluctance, conviction, patience, solving nothing, or creating a handle.
- The plot pattern of contemplating destruction, delaying with a drink, and returning with a changed perspective.
- The specific contrast between plants that seem informed and a person who is not.

### Surface manifestations — adapt deliberately

- Third-person narration, object personification, a long sentence followed by a very short one, concrete domestic imagery, a single attitude-bearing adverb, simile, and deadpan commentary.
- These devices are evidence of the taste, not a mandatory recipe. Onboarding will usually need second-person, imperative, or neutral UI language rather than miniature third-person scenes.
- Personification and figurative language may be used only when they clarify a state or soften friction. They should disappear on high-stakes, error, consent, fee, risk, and security surfaces.

### Portable principles — preserve

- Quiet animation grounded in functional clarity.
- Precise, modest language instead of grand emotional claims.
- Rhythm that alternates composed observation with brief undercut or release.
- Dry humor that protects the user's dignity.
- Indirect empathy: recognize hesitation without assigning an emotion or identity.
- Patience over urgency.
- Honest, bounded progress over transformation theater.

## Signature principles

1. **Make the ordinary faintly alive.** Choose concrete verbs that give a process presence while keeping its function obvious.
2. **Let cadence carry the wit.** Build a thought with measured syntax, then use a short line to alter its meaning or temperature.
3. **Keep the user on the inside of the joke.** The situation may be gently absurd; the person is never the target.
4. **Understate the feeling.** Show or accommodate uncertainty without naming a psychological state the product cannot know.
5. **Prefer a foothold to a victory lap.** Frame each step as useful and limited, with no false promise that setup equals resolution.
6. **Stay patiently observant.** Calm comes from unhurried language, low punctuation, and respect for the user's pace—not from blandness.

## Productive tensions

- calm, not sterile
- wry, not cute
- patient, not passive
- specific, not decorative
- human, not confessional
- brief, not abrupt
- reassuring, not falsely certain
- lightly surprising, not performative
- composed, not managerial

## Invariants

- Functional meaning remains clear on first read.
- The user is never mocked, diagnosed, shamed, or treated as financially naive.
- Progress claims are narrow and literally true.
- Wit stays sparse enough that it feels discovered rather than applied.
- Emotional language remains low-temperature and non-coercive.
- Sentence rhythm includes some contrast; the voice is not a stream of equally clipped fragments.
- No source-specific content, image, phrase, or narrative sequence survives.
- Accessibility, consent, financial accuracy, security, and regulatory clarity outrank stylistic fidelity.

## Degrees of freedom

- Use second person, neutral labels, or direct imperatives according to the product's established grammar; third-person narration is not required.
- Adjust sentence length to the component. Buttons and field labels can be entirely plain; helper text and transitions can carry more cadence.
- Use no humor where stakes are high. The taste can survive through restraint, precision, and honest scope alone.
- Choose details native to the real product flow, provided they explain function rather than decorate it.
- Use standard financial terminology when it is the clearest language; do not replace necessary terms with metaphor.
- Vary the amount of warmth by screen while keeping patience, dignity, and bounded claims constant.
- Punctuation, capitalization, contractions, and formal address may follow the app's existing design system.

## Anti-patterns and originality guardrails

- Do not use horticultural, mechanical, postal, disposal, beverage, dawn, or directional imagery from the source.
- Do not preserve the source's nouns while swapping adjectives, or paraphrase its memorable lines.
- Do not construct a miniature story that follows the source's event order.
- Do not equate “same style” with making every object sentient.
- Do not turn deadpan understatement into bleakness, sarcasm, or indifference.
- Do not make the app sound cleverer than the user.
- Do not add jokes to fees, identity checks, security, permissions, risk, errors, destructive actions, or legally significant statements.
- Do not use generic wellness language, triumphal financial language, or promises of control that the product cannot substantiate.
- Reject any line whose charm depends on recognizing the source passage.

## Downstream production handoff

### Target-specific rules

- Start from the screen's required action, consequence, and factual constraints; voice is a second pass.
- Keep headings and controls literal. Put any tonal nuance in optional helper text or transitions.
- Give each screen one main job and one modest next step.
- Use concrete verbs and low-intensity diction. Prefer a truthful limitation to an expansive reassurance.
- Permit uncertainty and reversible choices when the actual product supports them.
- Use a cadence contrast only where it improves orientation or reduces pressure.
- Keep wit sparse, situational, and directed away from the user.
- Remove stylistic flourishes from sensitive or high-stakes copy.

### Production sequence

1. Record the exact purpose of each onboarding screen, the action available, and the consequence of taking or skipping it.
2. Mark required legal, security, consent, accessibility, and product terminology before drafting.
3. Draft the shortest plain-language version that is factually complete and actionable.
4. Identify the likely friction without assigning the user an emotion or deficiency.
5. Add, where safe, one restrained taste move: a more exact verb, a small cadence turn, or a dry situational observation.
6. Cut hype, generic reassurance, unearned celebration, repetition, and any line that calls attention to the writing.
7. Run the originality, dignity, high-stakes, and factual-scope checks below in sequence.

### Rejection tests for shallow imitation

- **Noun-swap test:** Does the draft replace source objects with finance objects while preserving the same scene, comparison, or sentence skeleton? Reject it.
- **Costume test:** Does the draft rely on personification, domestic imagery, or a deadpan punchline without carrying patience, dignity, and bounded progress? Reject it.
- **Cleverness test:** Is the writer's joke more noticeable than the user's next action? Reject or flatten it.
- **Target test:** Could the copy fit any lifestyle app after changing one noun? Rewrite it from actual product facts.
- **Dignity test:** Is uncertainty framed as incompetence, irresponsibility, avoidance, or a personality flaw? Reject it.
- **Truth test:** Does setup appear to solve, secure, grow, simplify, or control finances beyond what the action demonstrably does? Narrow the claim.
- **Stakes test:** Is humor present in a consent, security, fee, risk, error, or destructive-action context? Remove it.
- **Source-recognition test:** Would a reader of the passage recognize a phrase, image, event sequence, or conspicuous cadence replica? Reject it.
